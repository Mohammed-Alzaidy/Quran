Electrical Harmonics in Circuits

Introduction

Electrical harmonics are voltages or currents that operate at a frequency that is an integer (whole-number) multiple of the fundamental frequency. For example, with a 50Hz fundamental frequency, we can expect harmonics at 100Hz, 150Hz, 200Hz, and so on. Harmonics are unwanted higher frequencies which superimpose on the fundamental waveform creating a distorted wave pattern.

Harmonics are generated by non-linear loads, which are electrical devices or circuits that have a voltage-current characteristic that is not linear, that is, the current flowing through them is not proportional to the applied voltage. Some examples of non-linear loads are iron-cored inductors, switching transformers, electronic ballasts in fluorescent lights, rectifiers, silicon controlled rectifiers (SCRs), power transistors, power converters and other solid state switches.

Harmonics can cause various problems in electrical power systems, such as overheating of transformers and cables, tripping of breakers and fuses, malfunctioning of meters and relays, interference with communication and control systems, reduction of power factor and efficiency, and increased losses and costs.

In this article, we will discuss some of the causes and effects of harmonics in electrical circuits, as well as some methods to measure and mitigate them.

Causes of Harmonics

As mentioned earlier, harmonics are produced by non-linear loads that draw current in abrupt pulses rather than in a smooth sinusoidal manner. These pulses cause harmonic currents to flow back into other parts of the power system, creating harmonic voltages across the impedances of the system.

One common source of harmonics is the rectifier circuit, which converts alternating current (AC) to direct current (DC). A rectifier circuit consists of diodes that allow current to flow only in one direction. When the AC voltage is positive, the diodes conduct and charge a capacitor. When the AC voltage is negative, the diodes block and the capacitor discharges. This results in a pulsating DC voltage with a ripple frequency that is twice the fundamental frequency.

In fact, it can be shown mathematically that the current waveform contains only odd harmonics (3rd, 5th, 7th, etc.) and that their magnitudes decrease as the harmonic order increases.

Another common source of harmonics is the switching circuit, which uses solid state devices such as transistors or SCRs to control the power delivered to a load. A switching circuit operates by turning on and off the devices at high frequencies to create a pulse width modulated (PWM) waveform. The PWM waveform can vary the average voltage and current delivered to the load by changing the duty cycle (the ratio of on time to off time) of the pulses.

As we can see, the voltage waveform is not sinusoidal but has abrupt transitions between high and low levels. This means that it contains harmonics of higher frequencies than the fundamental frequency. In fact, it can be shown mathematically that the voltage waveform contains both odd and even harmonics (2nd, 4th, 6th, etc.) and that their magnitudes depend on the duty cycle and switching frequency.

Effects of Harmonics

Harmonics can have various negative effects on electrical circuits and systems. Some of them are:

  • Overheating: Harmonic currents increase the effective resistance of conductors due to skin effect and proximity effect. Skin effect is the tendency of high frequency currents to flow near the surface of conductors rather than uniformly throughout their cross-section. Proximity effect is the tendency of high frequency currents to flow near each other rather than uniformly throughout their cross-section when they are close together. Both effects increase the power losses and heating in conductors.
  • Distortion: Harmonic voltages distort the shape of the voltage waveform, which can affect the performance of sensitive equipment such as computers, controllers, meters and relays. Harmonic currents distort the shape of the current waveform, which can increase the harmonic losses and reduce the power factor. Harmonic distortion can also cause resonance in capacitors, transformers and other reactive components, leading to overvoltages and overcurrents that can damage them.
  • Interference: Harmonics can interfere with communication and control systems that use power lines as transmission media, such as power line carrier (PLC), supervisory control and data acquisition (SCADA), and remote terminal unit (RTU). Harmonics can also interfere with radio and television signals that are transmitted or received near power lines.

Measurement of Harmonics

Harmonics can be measured using various instruments and methods. Some of them are:

  • Oscilloscope: An oscilloscope can display the voltage or current waveform on a screen and show its shape and amplitude. However, an oscilloscope cannot measure the frequency or magnitude of each harmonic component directly. It can only show the overall distortion of the waveform.
  • Spectrum Analyzer: A spectrum analyzer can display the frequency spectrum of a voltage or current waveform on a screen and show the magnitude of each harmonic component as a vertical bar. A spectrum analyzer can also measure the total harmonic distortion (THD), which is the ratio of the root mean square (RMS) value of all harmonic components to the RMS value of the fundamental component.
  • Power Quality Analyzer: A power quality analyzer is a specialized instrument that can measure various parameters related to power quality, such as voltage, current, frequency, power factor, harmonics, flicker, transients, and so on. A power quality analyzer can also record and store data for later analysis.

Mitigation of Harmonics

Harmonics can be mitigated using various devices and methods. Some of them are:

  • Harmonic Filter: A harmonic filter is a device that is used to reduce or eliminate harmonics from a power system. A harmonic filter can be either passive or active. A passive harmonic filter consists of passive components such as resistors, capacitors and inductors that are tuned to resonate at one or more harmonic frequencies and provide low impedance paths for harmonic currents to flow to ground. An active harmonic filter consists of active components such as transistors or SCRs that generate harmonic currents that are opposite in phase to the harmonic currents in the power system and cancel them out.
  • Isolation Transformer: An isolation transformer is a device that transfers electrical energy from one circuit to another without direct electrical connection. An isolation transformer can isolate a load from the source and reduce or eliminate harmonics by providing a high impedance path for harmonic currents to flow back to the source.
  • Phase Shifting Transformer: A phase shifting transformer is a device that shifts the phase angle of a voltage or current waveform by a certain amount. A phase shifting transformer can reduce or eliminate harmonics by shifting the phase angle of different loads so that their harmonic currents cancel each other out when they are added together.
  • Power Converter: A power converter is a device that converts one form of electrical energy to another, such as AC to DC, DC to AC, AC to AC, or DC to DC.A power converter can reduce or eliminate harmonics by using advanced switching techniques such as pulse width modulation (PWM), space vector modulation (SVM), or multilevel converters that produce less distorted output waveforms than conventional converters.

Conclusion

Electrical harmonics are voltages or currents that operate at higher frequencies than the fundamental frequency of the power system. Harmonics are generated by non-linear loads that draw current in abrupt pulses rather than in a smooth sinusoidal manner. Harmonics can cause various problems in electrical circuits and systems, such as overheating, distortion, interference, and resonance. Harmonics can be measured using instruments such as oscilloscopes, spectrum analyzers, and power quality analyzers. Harmonics can be mitigated using devices and methods such as harmonic filters, isolation transformers, phase shifting transformers, and power converters.
